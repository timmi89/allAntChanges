Awesome Skate Ramp Afloat On the Waters of Lake Tahoe

Awesome Skate Ramp Afloat On the Waters of Lake Tahoe

share this on twitter share this on facebook
Charlie Gilbert
Scribol Staff
Extreme Sports
twitter facebook pinterest

One man, however, remained undeterred by the challenge of how to transform a normally urban sport into a more aquatic activity. In 2013 pro skateboarder Bob Burnquist and a design and build team turned up at Lake Tahoe, armed with a cunning plan as to how skateboards and water could finally work together in harmony.

When he was approached by Visit California to participate in the organization’s Dream365 tourism promotion, the Brazilian-American skater got thinking about how the state could be promoted to prospective visitors, and he ultimately turned to one of his passions to do so.

Burnquist’s brief was based on two simple and perhaps slightly ambiguous concepts: thinking big and turning dreams into reality. As Burnquist has conquered practically every skateable surface the world over, he and his team of designers decided that skating on water was the next logical step – and so that’s exactly what they set about doing.

The seemingly improbable project was realized thanks to this 7,300-pound ramp – which, despite its immense weight, floated on Lake Tahoe’s beautiful turquoise waters. Impressively, the eye-catching structure took Burnquist’s team a mere four days, or 300 hours, to bring to fruition.

After the ramp was lowered into position, Burnquist – the man of the moment – was called upon for a test run. And it went swimmingly: Burnquist even culminated his run with a huge ollie into the lake.

Designed to feature a 45-degree ramp, a half pipe and a quarter pipe, the ramp was built with thrills, and perhaps spills, in mind. It was certainly geared up for some impressive moves, and thanks to weighted riggers, Burnquist didn’t have to worry about the ramp wobbling. A combination of an aluminum frame – originally intended to hold stage lights – and over a dozen plastic barrels were also incorporated to ensure that the ramp remained buoyant.

Such attention to detail likely went down well with Burnquist. Indeed, in a Visit California YouTube video on the project, the skater showed himself to be upbeat about the whole venture, speaking animatedly about his “opportunity to come out to a lake and skate.”

In the video, Burnquist adds, “Give me ideas and the freedom to manifest them and it’s, like… It’s awesome. I love it,” before going on to enthuse, “All my dreams came true in California.”

Waxing lyrical about dreams and making them come true, Burnquist explains in the clip that as a kid his overriding ambition was simply “to have a ramp in [his] backyard.” However, he says that he “had no idea that dream would be as big as it became.”

“All of a sudden I got this vert ramp, and I got a loop, and I got a corkscrew… and then there’s a mega ramp,” the skater adds, concluding that as far as his dream goes, “the beauty of it is that it hasn’t ended yet.” He’s still “living it, every day.”

Recognizable skateable features on the floating creation also added potential variety for Burnquist. These were of different heights: the half pipe measured five feet tall, while the jump ramp was slightly smaller at four feet, and the roll stretched to a full eight feet.

The entire platform itself was approximately 35 feet long and 12 feet wide – which proved big enough to keep Burnquist entertained for around three whole hours. No wonder he seems so happy in the Visit California YouTube feature.

The platform also boasted an attractive striped amber and mahogany finish, with the sleek design of the entire skate ramp the work of professional ramp constructor Jeff King and veteran production designer and art director Jerry Blohm.

However, while the building process was remarkably quick, the ramp’s design took a month to perfect. Judging solely on its stunning appearance, though, it was time well spent.

Construction commenced at Obexer’s Boat Company’s Marina on Lake Tahoe’s western shore, and once it was completed, the ramp was ready for action.

“Hopefully this thing floats good,” King says in the Visit California video, prior to the ramp being lowered onto the lake. Presumably addressing Burnquist, he adds, “Hopefully it doesn’t rock too much. Hopefully my bank gets you enough speed to launch you off that launch.”

Fortunately, King needn’t have worried. The rocking was indeed kept to a minimum – at least, to no more than a boat would rock – which meant that Burnquist was able to pull off some pretty impressive moves.

Keeping the ramp afloat was facilitated by a system of valves designed to keep the water out. “If they leak,” warned Blohm beforehand, “the thing will rest on the bottom and all of this work will be for nothing. Bob will be skating underwater.” As these images suggest, however, nothing of the sort happened. Burnquist and the ramp remained firmly buoyant.

A crew spent a couple of hours towing the ramp to the shores of D. L. Bliss State Park, during which time it received its “pirate ship” nickname – a nod to its boat-like curves and dark brown hues. The ramp was certainly an unusual sight and drew inquisitive looks from bystanders.

“We had a host of folks coming up to the ramp on the way out to see what it was exactly,” Blohm explained. “When they got close most could not believe it. It looks like it is fake, floating with no supports.”

One less serious, yet somewhat predictable, problem the crew faced was the issue of how to retrieve Burnquist’s board in the event of it hitting the water. The solution was to enlist a snorkeler whose job it was to fetch the board on any occasion that it landed in the lake.

Sadly, the skate ramp wasn’t destined for a long life on Lake Tahoe. After Burnquist’s tricks were filmed for Visit California, it was taken apart.

Greg Carson, chief creative officer and partner at MeringCarson – the agency behind Visit California’s Dream365 campaign – explained, “We didn’t want any kids riding on it or stealing our thunder by putting a video out.” Still, he added of the ramp, “The plans are intact, so we could build [it] again.”

However, while the ramp is no more, its memory lives on online. Intriguingly, of the 30 videos produced for the Dream365 campaign, it was the one featuring Burnquist skating atop the floating ramp on Lake Tahoe that turned out to be the standout crowdpleaser.
